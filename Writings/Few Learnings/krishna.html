About Rashmirathi


Rashmirathi, which has a literal meaning of “The Suns charioteer”, is an epic that retells the story of Karna, a significant character from the Indian epic, the Mahabharata.

The poem primarily revolves around the life of Karna, who is known as Surya-Putra (The son of the Sun god, Surya). It explorers Karna’s character, his struggles and his tragic destiny.

Dinkar presents Karna as a noble and tragic hero who faces numerous challenges and ethical dilemmas.  The poem delves into Karna’s loyalty, sense of honor, and his unwavering commitment to his principles.

Rashmirathi is not just a narration but also contains profound philosophical reflections on life, duty and destiny. Dinkar weaves philosophical elements in to the storyline, offering readers a deeper understanding of the human condition.

Dinkar’s poetic style in Rashmirathi is marked by its rich language and powerful versus. It creates a captivating and emotionally charge narrative of Karna’s complex and pivotal character in Mahabharata.

At its essence, Rashmirathi acknowledges the recognition of high qualities in an individual. It emphasizes that the true recognition of an individual’s elevated virtues can only be attained through one’s actions and conduct.

Rashmirathi is not only a poetry exploration of mythical character’s life, but also a work that addresses universal themes and resonates with readers for its literary and philosophical depth. The poem’s impact extends beyond literature, influencing discussions on morality, duty, and human values.

 

A Beacon of Social Transformation in a Caste-Entrenched Society


Rashmirathi gains increased relevance when viewed in the historical context of a society deeply entrenched in casteism. During the time when caste divisions were prevalent in Indian society, and people's lives were profoundly influenced by caste considerations, Dinkar's epic takes on a transformative role. The poem goes beyond the traditional boundaries set by caste and encourages a broader understanding of human values and character.

Despite being from an upper caste himself, Dinkar's work reflects a progressive and inclusive vision. He uses the character of Karna, who is traditionally considered to be from a lower caste, to challenge the rigid caste structures and prejudices. Karna's struggle and virtues, as portrayed in Rashmirathi, become a metaphor of breaking free from the shackles of caste-based discrimination.

Dinkar, through his poetry, motivates Indians to transcend the darkness of ignorance associated with casteism. He emphasizes the importance of recognizing individuals based on their merits, actions, and character rather than their caste background. The epic serves as a powerful call for unity, equality, and a collective rise above the divisive barriers of caste.

Rashmirathi becomes a literary tool to inspire social change, encouraging people to question and challenge the prevailing caste norms and prejudices. Dinkar's message resonates with a timeless appeal for a more inclusive and egalitarian society, urging individuals to rise above the limitations imposed by caste distinctions and focus on the shared humanity that binds them together.


About Ramdhari Singh Dinkar


Ramdhari Singh Dinkar, 23 Sept. 1908 - 24 Apr 1974, was an influential poet, essayist, and academic. He is considered one of the great Hindi poets of all time.

Dinkar was born in Simaria, a small village at the shore of the river Ganga in Bihar. He had the alma mater of the Patna University.

Dinkar’s poetry is known for its powerful and evocative verses. His works include not only poetry but also essays, plays, and critiques. Some of his notable works include Rashmirathi, Urvashi, Sanskriti Ke Chaar Adhyay. He was honoured with the Sahitya Akademy Award in 1959 for his work Sanskriti Ke Chaar Adhyay. He also received the Padma Bhushan, India's third highest civilian award in 1959.

Ramdhari Singh Dinkar’s legacy continues through his literary contributions, which have left a lasting impact on Hindi literature. His poems are still recited and admired and he is remembered as a poet who brought a unique blend of intellectual depth and emotional intensity to his work.


A Little Background on Mahabharata


The Mahabharata is one of the two major Sanskrit epics of ancient India, the other being the Ramayana. Attributed to the sage Vyasa, the Mahabharata is a colossal epic that consists of over 100,000 slokas (verses), and is divided into 18 sections.

The Mahabharata primarily revolves around the conflict between two groups of cousins, the Pandavas and the Kauravas. The central theme is the Kurukshetra was a great battle between these two factions.


Background

King Shantanu of Kuru dynasty and the ruler of Hastinapur marries the river goddess Ganga, who gives birth to Devavrata. After several tragic events, including the death of Shantanu's 7 children and the departure of Ganga, Shantanu wanted to marry Satyavati, whose father demanded that she will marry the king Shantanu only when the king promises to pass on the throne to Satyavati’s child that would be born after the marriage and then the lineage should pass on accordingly. But according to tradition, the throne must be passed to the eldest son if he is capable of ruling the empire and then the lineage should continue passing on.

King Shantanu refused this demand as he already had made his son Devavrata the prince of Hastinapura. But when Devavrata got to know about this from other sources that his father is sacrificing the wish to marry Satyavati, he went to Satyavati's father and took an oath known as the Bhishma Pratigya (Bhishma's vow). He vowed lifelong celibacy i.e. Brahmacharya, never to marry or have children and relinquished the right to the throne for the sake of his father's happiness and the stability of kingdom.

This selfless vow earned him the name Bhishma which means formidable. Bhishma's vow, while ensuring the stability of throne also set the stage for the complex events leading to the great holy war at Kurukshetra where Krishna would recite the Gita and Karna would sacrifice his life in the battlefield.

Now, a great link between Ramayana and Mahabharata can be seen here. After King Shantanu, the lineage of rulers in Hastinapur continued with his descendants.

Chitrangada, the elder son of Shantanu, succeeded him as the ruler of Hastinapur. However, he died young and had no heirs. With the death of Chitrangada, Shantanu's next son, Vichitravirya, ascended the throne. Vichitravirya married Ambika and Ambalika, but he also died without producing an heir. Now, as Vichitravirya had no heirs, Bhishma sought the help of sage Vyasadeva, who performed a special ritual to impregnate the queens, Ambika and Ambalika. During that ritual, some misfortunes happened, leading to Ambika giving birth to Dhritarashtra, who was blind from birth, and Ambalika giving birth to Pandu, who had health issues.

Dhritarashtra was the elder one, but since he was blind, Pandu was crowned as the king of Hastinapur. However, due to the curse of sage Kindama, Pandu was unable to father child naturally. His queens, Kunti and Madri, bore children through divine boons.

Dhritarashtra's sons were Kauravas (including Duryodhana, the main antagonist in Mahabharata, and 99 other sons), while Pandu's sons were the Pandavas (Yudhishthira, Bhima, Arjuna, Nakula and Sahadeva).

Pandu's reign took a tragic turn when he faced an untimely death. Consequently, Dhritarashtra, despite of being blind, was crowned as the king of Hastinapur.

The Mahabharata epic narrates the conflicts and events that unfolded between the Kauravas and Pandavas, leading to the great Kurukshetra war. The Kurukshetra war was a major conflict that took place between the Kauravas and the Pandavas, with Lord Krishna serving as the charioteer for Arjuna. The war resulted in the destruction of Kauravas and Pandavas emerged victorious.


About Karna

Kunti, the princess of Kunti kingdom, who later became the wife of Pandu, was given a special boon by the sage Durvasa during her premarital life. This boon granted her the ability to invoke any deity and have children by then. Curious about the power of boon, Kunti tested it and invoked the sun god, Surya. Karna is the son of Kunti and Surya and hence called Surya-Putra (The son of Surya).

Faced with the challenges of being an unwed mother, Kunti decided to place Karna in a basket and set him afloat on the river Ganga. The infant Karna was discovered and adopted by Adhiratha, a charioteer, and his wife Radha. Later on, Karna was also being known as Radhey (The son of Radha).

Karna, unaware of his royal lineage, grew up as the son of a charioteer. Despite facing societal prejudices due to his humble upbringing, Karna displayed exceptional skills and earned respect for his virtues and talents. Duryodhana, recognizing Karna's prowess, befriended him, offering him the throne of Anga Kingdom. Karna, grateful for the friendship and recognition denied by others, pledged his unwavering loyalty to Duryodhana.

Despite knowing his true lineage lately (That too from Kunti herself), and the opportunity to side with his half-brothers, the Pandavas, Karna chooses to fight for Duryodhana in the Kurukshetra war. His loyalty to his friend and his sense of duty prevails over familial bonds.

Karna's tragic fate becomes a symbol of consequences of societal vices and a poignant reflection on loyalty, identity, and destiny. Gradually and subtly, Rashmirathi brings Karma's fate into revelation.

 

The Righteous Struggle

In the context of Dharma, the Pandavas are often considered to represent righteousness. They faced numerous injustices, including the infamous game of dice in which they lost their kingdom, their exile in the forest, and various attempts on their lives. Lord Krishna served as a guide and advisor to the Pandavas.

On the other hand, the Kauravas, particularly led by Duryodhana, engaged in deceitful tactics and unfair strategies. The Kauravas' refusal to return the Pandavas their rightful share of the kingdom after their exile in the forest for 13 years, and their role in plotting against them, contribute to the growing animosity between the two factions.

Cheerharan, that has a literal meaning of disrobing, is a significant episode in Mahabharata where Draupadi, the wife of Pandavas, is humiliated and nearly disrobed by Dushasana, a Kaurava, in the Kauravas' court during the game of dice (Pasha). This degrading incident highlights the erosion of righteousness and becomes a turning point in the epic, leading to the eventual Kurukshetra war. Lord Krishna intervenes to protect Draupadi and guide the Pandavas through their trials.

 

Important Characters

  • Dronacharya:
  • Dronacharya was the royal teacher (guru) of Kuru princes, including the Pandavas and the Kauravas. Arjuna, one of the Pandavas' brothers, was Dronacharya's most notable pupil. He played a pivotal role in Kurukshetra war. Despite being a virtuous and skilled teacher, he fought on the side of Kauravas due to his loyalty to Hastinapur.
  • Kripacharya
  • Kripacharya, like Dronacharya, was an accomplished warrior and teacher. He was known for his expertise in various weapons and combat skills. Kripacharya was especially close to Kauravas, being their martial teacher and advisor.
  • Krishna
  • The eighth avatar of Lord Vishnu and central character in the Mahabharata, Krishna serves as the charioteer and advisor to Arjuna during the Kurukshetra war. He delivers the Bhagavad Gita, a discourse on duty and spirituality, addressing Arjuna's moral dilemmas that comes at the holy land of Kurukshetra when he realizes that he has to kill all of his cousins, teachers, grandparents, uncles, relatives for the throne of Hastinapur. Krishna, also plays a key role in diplomatic efforts trying to prevent the war, and his divine nature is revealed throughout the epic.
  • Gandhari
  • The wife of Dhritarashtra and the mother of Kauravas, she voluntarily blindfolds herself throughout her married life to share in her husband's blindness.
  • Shakuni
  • The maternal uncle of Kauravas and the master manipulator, he is the brother of Gandhari and he came along with her to Hastinapur after the marriage of her sister. Shakuni is often portrayed as the instigator of the conflict between the Kauravas and the Pandavas.
